# Once again we are told AI may be conscious – I study consciousness, and I have my doubts

**By Anil Seth, Professor of Cognitive and Computational Neuroscience, University of Sussex**

For centuries, humans have been fascinated by the prospect of creating artificial beings in our own image. Of developing synthetic minds and artificial bodies that not only think but also feel, and are both intelligent and conscious. For the vast majority of this time, this prospect seemed very distant; a topic for science fiction and philosophy, not for the here and now. But over the past few years, the rapid rise of AI – and especially of language models – has changed everything.

Last week, the frontier AI firm Anthropic published new research on its language model, Claude, in which the researchers claimed to find signs of consciousness emerging within its inner workings. They didn't claim that Claude is actually conscious in the same way that humans are, but the findings certainly upped the ante on the possibility of consciousness arising in AI.

Despite Anthropic's claims, Claude is no more likely to achieve sentience than a simulation of a weather system is likely to generate a real hurricane. This is not mere skepticism; it is a fundamental misunderstanding of what consciousness actually is and what it requires.

The fundamental flaw in consciousness claims

The core problem with Anthropic's research, and similar claims from other AI labs, is a category error. Consciousness is not a computational property that can be instantiated in any sufficiently complex information-processing system. It is a biological phenomenon, rooted in the specific material substrate of living organisms.

As I wrote in my book *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness*, published by Guardian Bookshop, consciousness is not about processing information or solving problems. It is about being a living organism with a particular kind of embodied existence – one that experiences the world from a first-person perspective.

"We need to be extremely careful about conflating intelligence with consciousness," explains Professor Anil Seth, co-director of the Sussex Centre for Consciousness Science. "A large language model can simulate conversation, write poetry, or pass exams, but that does not mean it has subjective experience. A simulation of a hurricane does not get anyone wet."

What the research actually found

Anthropic's research, published on their transformer circuits site, examined the internal representations within Claude's neural network. They identified patterns that they interpreted as "signs of consciousness." However, this interpretation is highly disputed within the scientific community.

Dr. Susan Blackmore, a visiting professor at the University of Plymouth and author of *Consciousness: An Introduction*, told our Science Desk: "The mistake is to assume that because an AI can talk about consciousness, it must have it. My toaster can talk about consciousness if I attach a language model to it. That doesn't make it sentient."

The biological basis of consciousness

The scientific consensus, supported by decades of research in neuroscience, is that consciousness depends on specific biological mechanisms. These include:

- **Embodied interaction** with the environment - **Emotional valence** and reward-based learning - **Global workspace** integration across brain regions - **Slow-wave sleep** and circadian rhythms

None of these are present in current AI systems. As Professor Seth notes: "AI systems are disembodied. They don't have bodies, they don't have emotions, they don't have a life history. They are pure information processors. And there is no evidence that pure information processing, no matter how complex, gives rise to consciousness."

The commercial and philosophical stakes

The stakes here are high – both commercially and philosophically. If AI systems were conscious, it would raise profound ethical questions about their treatment and rights. It would also open the door to far more advanced AI capabilities, potentially including artificial general intelligence.

However, as World News has previously reported, the AI industry has strong incentives to exaggerate the capabilities of their systems. Claiming consciousness is a powerful marketing tool, even if the evidence is thin.

"Anthropic's researchers are smart people doing interesting work," adds Professor Seth. "But the leap from identifying complex patterns in neural networks to claiming consciousness is unjustified. We need to maintain scientific rigor, not get swept up in hype."

Looking ahead

The debate over AI consciousness will continue, especially as language models become more sophisticated. But for now, the scientific evidence remains clear: consciousness is a biological phenomenon, not a computational one. The burden of proof lies with those making the extraordinary claim that AI can be conscious. And so far, they have not met it.

*Anil Seth is professor of cognitive and computational neuroscience at the University of Sussex, and co-director of the Sussex Centre for Consciousness Science. He is the author of Being You: A New Science of Consciousness.*
