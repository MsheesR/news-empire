# First patients enrolled in record-breaking Ebola treatment trial in DRC

**KINSHASA, DRC** — In a landmark achievement for global health emergency response, the first patients have been enrolled in a record-breaking clinical trial for experimental Ebola treatments in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, just six weeks after the outbreak was declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern by the World Health Organization on 17 May. Scientists and medical teams on the ground say the rapid setup of this trial, conducted in the volatile Ituri region, marks the fastest deployment of a randomized controlled treatment study in the history of Ebola outbreaks, offering a critical window to reduce the disease's historically high mortality rate.

The trial, which is testing two investigational antiviral drugs, was launched amid a surge in cases that has overwhelmed local health infrastructure. According to the WHO, the current outbreak has already recorded more than 450 confirmed cases and a case fatality rate exceeding 55% in some treatment centers. The study aims to enroll at least 700 patients across multiple sites in Ituri and North Kivu provinces, with interim results expected within the next three to six months.

Two drugs under investigation in a race against time

The double-blind, randomized trial is evaluating the efficacy of two experimental compounds: a monoclonal antibody cocktail developed by a consortium of international research institutes and a novel oral antiviral agent designed to inhibit viral replication. Neither drug has yet received regulatory approval for the treatment of Ebola. The trial is being conducted under the oversight of the DRC's National Institute for Biomedical Research and the Science Desk, with support from the WHO and the U.S. National Institutes of Health.

“We are enrolling patients at a pace that would have been unthinkable during the West Africa outbreak a decade ago,” said Dr. Anne-Marie Kabange, lead investigator of the trial and director of infectious disease research at the University of Kisangani. “The outbreak was declared a public health emergency on 17 May. By late June, we had ethical approvals, drug supplies, and trained staff in place. This is the gold standard for emergency research.”

The rapid mobilization was made possible by pre-positioned trial protocols and a dedicated cold-chain logistics network for the monoclonal antibody, which must be stored at minus 80 degrees Celsius. The oral antiviral, by contrast, requires only standard refrigeration, making it more suitable for remote rural clinics.

Challenges of operating in a conflict zone

The Ituri region, which borders Uganda and has been the epicenter of the current outbreak, is also a zone of active armed conflict. Health workers have faced attacks on treatment centers, community mistrust, and logistical hurdles that have delayed case identification and contact tracing.

“Operating a rigorous clinical trial in a conflict zone is an extraordinary logistical and ethical challenge,” said Dr. Jean-Pierre Mwamba, director of the DRC's National Ebola Response Coordination. “But the moral imperative is clear. We cannot afford to wait for a stable environment to test treatments. Every day we delay, people die.”

The trial design includes provisions for community engagement, informed consent procedures adapted for low-literacy populations, and mobile laboratory units capable of processing viral load samples within 24 hours. Researchers have also integrated an adaptive statistical framework that allows the Data Safety Monitoring Board to halt or modify the trial early if one drug demonstrates clear superiority or toxicity.

A turning point for emergency outbreak research

The speed of this trial's launch is being hailed by global health experts as a potential template for future pandemic preparedness. Historically, Ebola treatment trials during the 2014-2016 West Africa outbreak took months to initiate, and many were criticized for their poor design and lack of randomization.

“This is a paradigm shift. We have moved from 'let's try anything' to 'let's test everything rigorously' in the middle of a crisis,” said Dr. Paul Farmer, co-founder of Partners In Health and a professor of global health at Harvard University. “The DRC government and its partners have shown that it is possible to conduct high-quality science even under the most difficult circumstances. The results of this trial will not only save lives now but will inform how we respond to the next outbreak.”

The WHO has designated the outbreak a World News priority, with the United Nations mobilizing additional resources for containment. Meanwhile, the trial's success could accelerate regulatory pathways for these drugs, potentially making them available for future outbreaks in other endemic regions.

Looking ahead: What the trial means for global health

If the trial demonstrates a significant reduction in mortality—defined as a 30% or greater decrease in the case fatality rate—it could fundamentally alter the standard of care for Ebola. Currently, treatment is limited to supportive care, including rehydration and symptom management. An effective antiviral would give clinicians a direct weapon against the virus.

“We are cautiously optimistic,” said Dr. Kabange. “But we must also remember that the best treatment for Ebola is prevention. Vaccination efforts must continue in parallel. This trial is one piece of a larger puzzle.”

The study is expected to complete enrollment by early 2027, with final results published in a peer-reviewed journal. The Finance Desk notes that the trial is funded through a combination of public health grants and philanthropic donations, totaling approximately $45 million.

As the outbreak continues to spread, the eyes of the global health community remain fixed on the Ituri region. Whether this record-breaking trial will produce a lifesaving breakthrough remains to be seen, but its very existence represents a new chapter in the fight against one of the world's deadliest diseases.
