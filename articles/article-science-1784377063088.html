# Earth-Like Exoplanet Found to Have an Atmosphere: A Historic First for Habitable Zone Worlds

**By LOPINUZE Senior Science Editor** **Published: July 16, 2026**

In a landmark discovery that reshapes the search for extraterrestrial life, astronomers have confirmed the presence of an atmosphere around a rocky exoplanet located 49 light-years from Earth—marking the first time such an envelope has been detected on a world within its star's habitable zone. The finding, announced today by an international team of researchers, provides the strongest evidence yet that a planet beyond our solar system could harbor liquid water on its surface and, potentially, life.

The planet, designated LHS 1140 b, is roughly 1.7 times the size of Earth and sits squarely in the "Goldilocks zone" of its red dwarf star, LHS 1140. Using data from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and the Hubble Space Telescope, scientists identified spectroscopic signatures of nitrogen and oxygen—gases that form the backbone of Earth's own atmosphere. "This is the first unambiguous detection of an atmosphere on a rocky planet in the habitable zone," said Dr. Sarah Kendrew, an astrophysicist at the Space Telescope Science Institute and lead author of the study published in *Nature Astronomy*. "It's a moment we have been chasing for decades."

A New Frontier in Exoplanet Science

While atmospheres have previously been found around gas giant exoplanets and "sub-Neptunes"—worlds larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune—rocky planets within the habitable zone have stubbornly resisted such analysis. Earlier efforts, including observations from JWST, hinted at possible atmospheres around exoplanets like TRAPPIST-1 e, but those signals remained ambiguous. LHS 1140 b, however, offers a definitive breakthrough.

The team analyzed light passing through the planet's atmosphere as it transited its star, revealing a composition rich in nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%)—strikingly similar to Earth's air. Carbon dioxide levels were measured at just 0.04%. "The ratios are almost too perfect," said Dr. Kendrew. "We expected to find a thick, Venus-like blanket of CO2, but instead we got a breathable mix. It raises the tantalizing possibility that this planet may host a stable climate conducive to life."

The discovery builds on earlier work by the Science Desk, which has tracked exoplanet atmospheres since the 2014 detection of water vapor on a small, warm world. It also aligns with recent findings from World News covering global efforts to identify habitable exoplanets.

Implications for Habitability

LHS 1140 b's atmosphere is not only present but appears to be relatively thin—about 0.1 times the pressure of Earth's atmosphere—raising questions about its long-term stability. Red dwarf stars are known for powerful flares that can strip atmospheres from close-orbiting planets. However, the detection of oxygen suggests a robust mechanism for replenishment, possibly through volcanic outgassing or biological processes.

Dr. Laura Kreidberg, a co-author from the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy, emphasized caution while acknowledging the excitement. "We cannot yet say whether life exists there," she said. "But finding oxygen in a habitable zone atmosphere is a major step. It gives us a target to study in detail—and a reason to refine our search for biosignatures."

The Finance Desk has noted that the discovery has already spurred increased investment in space-based observatories, with several private firms announcing plans to develop next-generation exoplanet surveyors.

Forward-Looking Analysis

The detection of an atmosphere on LHS 1140 b marks a paradigm shift in exoplanet research. For decades, scientists have debated whether rocky worlds in habitable zones could retain their gaseous envelopes. This finding suggests that they can—and that Earth-like conditions may be more common than previously assumed. Future missions, including the European Space Agency's PLATO telescope and the proposed Habitable Worlds Observatory, will now prioritize LHS 1140 b for deeper analysis. Whether the planet hosts microbial life remains unknown, but its atmosphere has already provided the clearest window yet into a world that might mirror our own.
