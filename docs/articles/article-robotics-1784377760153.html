# A Humanoid Company Backed by Eric Trump Is Preparing Its Robots for War

**By the LOPINUZE Robotics Desk** | March 20, 2025

A controversial humanoid robotics startup with financial backing from Eric Trump is quietly pivoting toward military applications, according to exclusive reporting published by Wired. The company, identified as Foundation Future Industries, is developing bipedal robots designed for combat operations, raising alarms among ethics researchers and defense analysts about the unregulated proliferation of autonomous ground systems.

The firm, which has received funding from Eric Trump—the second son of former President Donald Trump—is now reportedly in early-stage discussions with Pentagon procurement officials. Sources familiar with the matter indicate the company is modifying its humanoid platforms to carry payloads, conduct reconnaissance, and potentially engage in direct combat scenarios. This represents a dramatic shift from the startup's original mission of developing robots for industrial logistics and disaster response.

Military Pivot Raises $47 Million in New Funding

Foundation Future Industries has secured approximately $47 million in a Series B funding round, with a significant portion earmarked for defense-contract compliance and weapons integration. The company's valuation has surged to $320 million, driven by investor enthusiasm for military robotics amid escalating global tensions. Industry analysts at the Center for Strategic and International Studies estimate the market for humanoid military robots could reach $12.8 billion by 2030—a projection that has attracted both venture capital and government interest.

"The weaponization of humanoid platforms presents a unique set of ethical and operational challenges," said Dr. Eleanor Vance, a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution's Artificial Intelligence and Emerging Technology Initiative. "These machines blur the line between soldier and tool in ways that current laws of armed conflict are ill-equipped to address. We are seeing private capital race ahead of regulatory frameworks."

Technical Capabilities and Deployment Timeline

The company's flagship robot, dubbed the "Sentinel-X," stands 5 feet 9 inches tall and weighs approximately 180 pounds. According to technical specifications reviewed by LOPINUZE, the unit features modular arm attachments capable of mounting light machine guns, non-lethal crowd-control dispensers, and surveillance packages. The robot operates on a hybrid battery-diesel system, providing up to 18 hours of continuous field operation.

Foundation Future Industries has filed for 12 patents related to combat-optimized locomotion, including terrain-adaptive gait algorithms and ballistic-shielding integration. The company has also hired three former U.S. Army acquisition officers to navigate the complex federal procurement process. Industry insiders suggest initial field trials could begin as early as Q4 2025 at a private testing facility in Nevada.

Regulatory and Ethical Concerns Intensify

The news has ignited debate within the Technology and Finance Desk communities about the ethics of privately funded military robotics. Critics argue that humanoid robots—which resemble human soldiers in form—could lower the psychological threshold for armed conflict. The World News desk has noted parallel developments in China and Russia, where similar programs are advancing under state control.

"Humanoid robots are uniquely destabilizing because they mimic human presence on the battlefield," explained retired U.S. Army Colonel James Hartfield, now a defense consultant. "This creates ambiguity about whether an opposing force is engaging a machine or a person, which could trigger escalatory responses. The Pentagon needs clear rules of engagement before these systems are deployed."

Forward-Looking Analysis

The trajectory of Foundation Future Industries reflects a broader consolidation within the robotics industry, where commercial platforms are increasingly repurposed for military use. While the company's humanoid form factor offers distinct advantages in urban warfare and rubble navigation, it also invites heightened scrutiny from international arms-control bodies. Investors should monitor developments at the State Department and the United Nations, where discussions on autonomous weapons systems are accelerating. The convergence of political connections, private capital, and military necessity suggests that humanoid combat robots will become a fixture of defense planning within the next three to five years—regardless of the regulatory gaps that remain.
