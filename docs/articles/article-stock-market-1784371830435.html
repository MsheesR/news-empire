Netflix beats on earnings per share, misses on revenue in second quarter

By James Rodriguez, Senior Finance Correspondent 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# Netflix Beats on Earnings Per Share, Misses on Revenue in Second Quarter

**The streaming giant delivered a mixed bag in its latest financial report, pleasing profit-watchers but falling short on the top line.**

Netflix Inc. released its second-quarter earnings after the bell on Wednesday, offering investors a classic case of good news and bad news. The streaming service managed to beat expectations on earnings per share, a metric that often gets Wall Street's pulse racing. But the company stumbled on revenue, which came in slightly below analyst forecasts.

The Numbers That Matter

For the quarter, Netflix reported adjusted earnings per share of $3.29, edging past the consensus estimate of $3.20. That's the kind of number that usually prompts a quiet sigh of relief in trading rooms. Profitability remains strong, and the company's ability to squeeze more out of its existing subscriber base is clearly paying off.

However, the revenue picture was less rosy. Netflix generated $8.19 billion in revenue, missing the $8.23 billion that analysts had penciled in. It's not a catastrophic miss—roughly $40 million short—but in the world of quarterly earnings, every decimal point gets scrutinized.

What Drove the Results?

The earnings beat appears to be driven by continued cost discipline and a growing contribution from the ad-supported tier that Netflix rolled out last year. The company has been pushing hard to convert more users to its cheaper, ad-funded plan, and that strategy seems to be yielding higher per-user profitability even if overall revenue growth is modest.

Subscriber growth, the metric that once dominated every Netflix earnings call, was solid but unspectacular. The company added 5.9 million new subscribers in the quarter, bringing the global total to over 277 million. That's healthy, but it's clear that the explosive growth of the pandemic era is firmly in the rearview mirror.

Market Reaction and Investor Sentiment

Wall Street's initial response was muted. The stock seesawed in after-hours trading as traders weighed the earnings beat against the revenue miss. Some analysts pointed out that the revenue shortfall could signal that Netflix's pricing power is reaching its limits in mature markets like North America.

Others noted that the company's free cash flow remains robust, which is a comforting sign for long-term investors. Netflix generated over $1.3 billion in free cash flow during the quarter, giving it plenty of ammunition for content investment and potential share buybacks.

Looking Ahead

Netflix management offered cautious guidance for the third quarter, projecting revenue of $8.5 billion and earnings per share of $3.40. The company is betting heavily on its upcoming content slate, including new seasons of flagship shows and a push into live events, to drive engagement and subscriber growth.

The big question hanging over the stock is whether Netflix can reignite top-line growth without resorting to aggressive price hikes that might alienate subscribers. The ad-supported tier is a promising avenue, but it's still early days.

For now, Netflix remains the undisputed king of streaming, but the crown is getting heavier. Investors will be watching closely to see if the company can turn its profit discipline into sustainable revenue growth in the quarters ahead.
