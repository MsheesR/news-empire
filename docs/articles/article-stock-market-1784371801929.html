What disruptive stocks are you holding?

By James Rodriguez, Senior Finance Correspondent 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# What Disruptive Stocks Are You Holding? One Investor's Cynical Bet on Human Nature

In a recent discussion that has sparked considerable debate among market watchers, one investor laid out a blunt thesis for their portfolio: bet on human laziness.

The investor, who goes by the handle Faceless_213, isn't shy about admitting their strategy leans on a less-than-flattering view of human behavior. "I think a lot about the companies that are changing the world," they wrote. "Companies like Uber, Lyft, and DoorDash are a HUGE portion of my portfolio."

The Convenience Economy

The logic is simple, if somewhat cynical. "It may be cynical, but I think people are lazy," the investor explained. "I know too many people who DoorDash multiple times a week just so they don't need to get out of their pajamas. I know even more people who are too intimidated by driving that they just depend on Uber."

This isn't exactly a new insight, but it does raise a compelling question for investors: Are we underestimating how much people will pay for convenience? The numbers suggest not. DoorDash alone reported over $2 billion in revenue in the most recent quarter, and Uber's mobility segment continues to grow as ride-hailing becomes a default mode of transportation in major cities.

The Therapy Bet

But the portfolio doesn't stop at food delivery and rides. The investor also holds a significant position in Teladoc Health (TDOC), the telemedicine company that has been beaten down substantially from its pandemic-era highs.

"I know it's beat down right now, but I still think it's a strong concept, at least the BetterHelp side," they noted, referring to Teladoc's online therapy platform. "My cynicism is showing again. I just don't think people put the effort into maintaining relationships. They need someone to talk to. And that someone? That's gonna be their therapist! They are going to spend a lot of money to vent to a professional."

It's a bet that seems to be paying off. The mental health market is projected to grow to over $500 billion by 2030, and online therapy platforms are capturing an increasing share of that spending.

What About the Big Picture?

The investor also acknowledged the difficulty of betting on other world-changing sectors. "I know green energy was (may still be?) a thing. I know nothing about it. If we find a way to do renewable energy, that's awesome! But at the same time, I just don't feel comfortable trying to figure out what works and what doesn't."

This highlights a crucial challenge for retail investors: how do you bet on transformative technology when you can't evaluate the underlying science? The investor mentioned GLP-1 drugs, the class of weight-loss medications that have taken the pharmaceutical world by storm, but admitted uncertainty about what else might be brewing in the pharma space.

The Takeaway

The post has resonated with many investors who share a similar worldview. While the strategy is undeniably cynical, it's also practical. Betting on human nature—our desire for convenience, our need for connection, and our tendency to outsource difficult tasks—has historically been a winning strategy.

As one commenter put it, "Sometimes the simplest thesis is the best one."

What disruptive stocks are you holding? The answer might say more about how you view human nature than about the companies themselves.
