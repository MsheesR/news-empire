The Most Brutal High Beta Momentum Sell-Off Ever Recorded?

By James Rodriguez, Senior Finance Correspondent 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# The Most Brutal High Beta Momentum Sell-Off Ever Recorded?

Wall Street has seen its fair share of bloodbaths, but the current sell-off in high beta momentum stocks is rewriting the history books—and not in a good way. According to market data that's leaving even seasoned traders scratching their heads, this downturn is shaping up to be the worst of its kind ever recorded.

A Gut Punch Worse Than 2008

Let that sink in for a moment. We're talking worse than the Great Financial Crisis. Worse than the COVID crash. Worse than the dot-com bubble collapse. And yes, even worse than the infamous tariff tantrum that sent markets into a tailspin a few years back.

I know that sounds almost impossible. The numbers, however, tell a story that's genuinely difficult to wrap your head around. There simply aren't enough words in the trading lexicon to properly describe how extreme and unusual this sell-off has been.

When Did the Hammer Drop?

The carnage started in late June and hasn't let up since. What began as a rough patch has morphed into relentless selling pressure across high beta and momentum stocks. Day after day, these names have been getting hammered with a consistency that feels almost mechanical.

What makes this particularly baffling? There's no single, clear fundamental reason that explains the sheer magnitude of this move.

Sure, the Iran deal fell through, but that wasn't exactly a shock to anyone paying attention. Rate hikes remain a possibility this year. The Iran conflict shows no signs of winding down. And historically, this period during a presidential midterm cycle tends to be weak for equities anyway.

But here's the thing: none of these factors explain why high beta stocks are getting punished more aggressively now than they were during the dot-com implosion. That's not hyperbole—that's the data.

The Numbers Don't Lie

The Goldman Sachs High Beta Momentum Index, a key benchmark for tracking these kinds of stocks, was down approximately 27% month-to-date through the first half of July. That represents its worst performance since the index was created.

Twenty-seven percent. In a matter of weeks.

To put that in perspective, during the COVID crash—when the entire world essentially shut down overnight—the selling pressure on these stocks wasn't this intense. During the 2008 financial crisis, when banks were collapsing like dominoes, it wasn't this bad either.

What Comes Next?

The million-dollar question, of course, is whether this is a buying opportunity or a warning sign of even more pain ahead. For every trader who sees this as a chance to scoop up beaten-down names at a discount, there's another who believes the selling has further to run.

One thing is certain: when you're breaking records that have stood through every major crisis of the past two decades, you're not in normal territory anymore. Whether that means we're due for a violent snapback or the beginning of a longer-term trend shift remains to be seen.

For now, all we can do is watch the numbers and wonder what comes next.
