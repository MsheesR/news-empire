TSMC beat every number and raised its outlook. Netflix's numbers were basically fine too. Both stocks got hit today anyway, for opposite reasons.

By James Rodriguez, Senior Finance Correspondent 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# TSMC beat every number and raised its outlook. Netflix's numbers were basically fine too. Both stocks got hit today anyway, for opposite reasons.

It was a brutal day on Wall Street for two of the most closely watched names in tech—but for completely different reasons. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (TSMC) turned in a quarter that would make most CEOs weep with joy. Netflix, meanwhile, delivered results that were "fine," which in today's market might as well be a four-letter word.

Yet both stocks got hammered. Here's what happened and why.

TSMC: The Victim of Its Own Success

Let's start with the chipmaking giant. TSMC posted record profit—up a staggering 77% year over year. Revenue and margins beat expectations. The company even raised its full-year growth forecast to over 40%. On paper, this is the kind of quarter that sends a stock soaring.

So why did shares fall?

The culprit was spending. TSMC also announced significantly higher capital expenditure plans. Investors, ever short-sighted, read that as near-term margin pressure. Never mind that the demand behind those investments has never looked stronger. The market's message was clear: "We love your growth, but we hate what it costs to get there."

As one analyst put it, "TSMC is being punished for spending aggressively to keep up with demand that doesn't show any signs of slowing. It's the ultimate first-world problem for a company."

Netflix: The Slow Fade

Then there's Netflix. The streaming giant essentially met expectations: a tiny earnings-per-share beat, revenue that came in a hair light. Nothing catastrophic. But the market wasn't interested in "fine."

The stock dropped nearly 9% and hit a new 52-week low. The culprit this time? Weaker forward guidance and a growth rate that keeps decelerating. Netflix was already down more than 20% for the year before today's bloodbath. Now it looks even worse.

The contrast with TSMC couldn't be starker. Netflix isn't being punished for spending too much. It's being punished for not growing fast enough. Its core business—adding subscribers in mature markets—is running out of steam. And while the company has tried everything from password-sharing crackdowns to ad-tier launches, the market is losing patience.

Two Stories, One Outcome

Same day. Same outcome. Opposite reasons.

TSMC is a company investing heavily in a future that looks incredibly bright. Netflix is a company trying to squeeze growth out of a business model that's hitting a ceiling.

Which kind of guidance would actually worry you more as an investor: a company spending heavily to keep up with demand, or one that's just running out of growth?

For now, the market seems to hate both. But the long-term story couldn't be more different. TSMC's pain looks temporary. Netflix's looks structural.

Time will tell which one the market is wrong about.
