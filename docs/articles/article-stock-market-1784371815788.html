Trump's media company wants traders to pay to get president's market-moving social-media posts first

By James Rodriguez, Senior Finance Correspondent 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# Trump's Media Company Wants Traders to Pay to Get President's Market-Moving Social-Media Posts First

In a move that's raising eyebrows across Wall Street and beyond, Trump Media & Technology Group is reportedly exploring a plan that would allow traders to pay for early access to President Trump's social media posts — before they go public. The proposal, first reported by MarketWatch, has sparked immediate controversy, with critics questioning both its legality and its implications for market fairness.

A New Pay-to-Play Model?

According to the report, the company behind Truth Social is considering a subscription-based service that would give paying traders a head start on the President's often market-moving pronouncements. The idea is simple: those willing to pay a premium would receive Trump's posts moments before they're published to the general public, potentially allowing them to trade on the information before the rest of the market reacts.

For a President whose tweets and Truth Social posts have historically sent stocks soaring or plummeting within seconds, the potential for profit — or loss — is enormous. Think of the times a single post about a company or industry has triggered massive sell-offs or buying frenzies. Now imagine having a 30-second or one-minute head start.

Legal Questions Loom Large

"I don't know why I'm surprised, but this seems highly illegal," one Reddit user commented on the r/StockMarket thread discussing the news. That sentiment appears widespread among retail investors who feel the system is already stacked against them.

Securities lawyers are already weighing in. The core legal question centers on whether this would constitute a form of insider trading or violate fair disclosure rules. Under Regulation Fair Disclosure (Reg FD), companies are generally prohibited from selectively disclosing material information to certain investors before making it public. While the President isn't technically a public company, his social media posts have repeatedly moved markets — and a paywall for early access could be seen as creating a two-tiered information system.

The Twilight Zone of Modern Markets

At what point do investors lose faith? That's the question many are asking. One commenter captured the mood perfectly: "I feel like we're living in the twilight zone."

The proposal touches on a deeper anxiety in today's markets: the growing gap between those with information advantages and everyone else. High-frequency traders already pay for milliseconds of speed advantage through co-location services. But this feels different — more personal, more direct, and tied directly to the most powerful person in the world.

A New Normal or a Bridge Too Far?

Supporters might argue that this is simply the latest evolution of information monetization. News organizations have long offered premium tiers for early access. And Trump's media company, struggling to turn a profit, needs new revenue streams.

But critics see something more troubling: a President potentially profiting from his own market-moving statements, creating what one trader called "a pay-to-play information gap that undermines the very idea of fair markets."

The question remains: Is this the new normal, or will regulators step in before the first subscription goes live? For now, traders are left watching their screens and wondering if they'll soon be watching someone else's screen — one that costs money to see.
