National Academies Report Backs Climate Change Attribution Science

By Prof. David Kim, Science & Space Editor 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# National Academies Report Backs Climate Change Attribution Science

When Hurricane Harvey dumped more than 50 inches of rain on Houston in 2017, devastating thousands of homes and leaving dozens dead, scientists were already working on a new kind of detective work. They wanted to know: How much of this was simply bad luck, and how much was a direct consequence of a warming planet?

Now, a major report from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine has given that line of inquiry a powerful endorsement. The panel of experts concluded that climate change attribution science—the ability to link specific extreme weather events to human-caused global warming—is advancing at a remarkable pace. And that progress, researchers say, could have profound implications far beyond the laboratory.

A Growing Field of Proof

For years, scientists could speak only in generalities: Climate change, they would say, makes heat waves more likely and hurricanes wetter. But the latest generation of attribution studies has become far more precise. Researchers can now calculate, with increasing confidence, how many times more likely a given storm, drought, or wildfire became because of rising greenhouse gas emissions.

"Attribution science is advancing quickly," the report notes. And that speed matters, because it is closing the gap between what the public experiences and what science can prove.

The panel, which included leading climate scientists and statisticians, emphasized that these methods are no longer experimental. They have matured to the point where they can be applied consistently and credibly to a wide range of extreme events.

Legal Shockwaves Ahead

But the report’s significance extends well beyond the realm of academic journals. As attribution science gains credibility, it is increasingly finding its way into courtrooms. Lawyers and plaintiffs are eyeing these studies as potential ammunition for lawsuits seeking damages from fossil fuel companies, utilities, and even governments.

Imagine a coastal city suing an oil company, arguing that its products contributed to sea-level rise that worsened flooding during a hurricane. Or a farmer in the Midwest seeking compensation for drought losses, pointing to studies showing that climate change made the dry spell substantially more severe.

That could support lawsuits seeking damages for severe events worsened by global warming, the report acknowledges. The key, researchers say, is that attribution studies can now provide the causal chain that legal systems demand: a direct, quantifiable link between emissions and harm.

Not a Silver Bullet

Still, experts caution that the path from scientific evidence to legal victory is not straightforward. Courts have historically been reluctant to assign liability for climate damages, citing issues of jurisdiction, causation, and the sheer number of actors involved.

Attribution science can show that a given event was more likely or intense because of climate change. But proving that a specific company's emissions were responsible for a specific flood or fire remains a formidable challenge.

Nevertheless, the National Academies report marks a turning point. It signals that the science has arrived—and that the debate is no longer about whether attribution is possible, but how to use it wisely.

As one researcher put it, "We now have the tools. The question is what we do with them."
