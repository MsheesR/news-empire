Meet Earth’s Newest Monkey. (And Check Out Its Orange Lips.)

By Prof. David Kim, Science & Space Editor 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

**Meet Earth’s Newest Monkey. (And Check Out Its Orange Lips.)**

It’s a rare moment in primatology when a brand-new monkey species is introduced to the world—rarer still when that monkey comes from Africa. In fact, this discovery marks only the fifth new African monkey species identified in the past 75 years.

**A Striking Newcomer**

Meet the *Cercopithecus lomamiensis*, a medium-sized monkey with a face that’s hard to forget. Its most noticeable feature? A pair of vivid, orange-red lips that stand out against its dark grayish-brown fur. The locals in the Democratic Republic of Congo have known about this animal for years, calling it “lesula.” But for scientists, verifying it as a distinct species required years of fieldwork, genetic analysis, and a bit of luck.

“It’s not every day you stumble upon a primate that’s been hiding in plain sight,” says Dr. John Hart, a biologist with the Lukuru Wildlife Research Foundation. Hart and his team first spotted the lesula in 2007 in a remote forest region of central DRC. At first, they thought it might be a juvenile of a known species. But its unique coloring—especially those lips—and its distinctive call patterns suggested otherwise.

**A Slow Path to Confirmation**

Identifying a new monkey isn’t as simple as snapping a photo. Hart’s team spent years collecting fecal samples, observing behavior, and comparing DNA with other guenons, a group of Old World monkeys. The genetic analysis confirmed what their eyes had already told them: this was a separate species.

The lesula has a long, dog-like snout and a tuft of hair on its crown. Its orange lips are not just cosmetic; they may play a role in social signaling, though researchers are still studying that. “Think of it as nature’s way of saying, ‘I’m different,’” Hart explains. “It’s a conversation starter, even among monkeys.”

**Why This Matters**

The discovery is more than a footnote in a biology textbook. It’s a reminder that vast, unexplored ecosystems still exist, even in the 21st century. The lesula’s home range is a fragmented forest in central Congo, an area increasingly threatened by bushmeat hunting and logging. Conservationists worry that the monkey’s newfound fame could also attract unwanted attention.

“We’re balancing excitement with caution,” says Dr. Kate Detwiler, a primatologist at Florida Atlantic University who also worked on the identification. “Announcing a new species brings attention, but it also puts a target on its back. We need to protect its habitat before it’s too late.”

**What’s Next**

For now, the lesula remains a mystery in many ways. Researchers are eager to learn about its social structure, diet, and range. But time is not on their side. The forests of the Congo Basin are shrinking, and each new species discovery underscores what we stand to lose.

“This monkey has been here all along,” Hart says. “It just took us a while to see it. Now that we do, the question is whether we can keep it around.”
