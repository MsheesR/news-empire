Researchers Claim to Have Solved the Perplexing ‘Reverse Sprinkler’ Problem That Stumped Feynman

By Prof. David Kim, Science & Space Editor 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# Researchers Claim to Have Solved the Perplexing ‘Reverse Sprinkler’ Problem That Stumped Feynman

**For decades, the question seemed almost childishly simple: If you take a standard lawn sprinkler and reverse the flow of water so it sucks water in instead of spraying it out, which way would it spin? The answer, it turns out, was anything but obvious.**

Physicists have long debated this very question, a puzzle that famously confounded even the brilliant Richard Feynman. Now, a team of researchers says they’ve finally settled the debate with careful, real-world experiments that provide a definitive answer.

A Puzzle from the Garden Hose

To understand the problem, picture a classic lawn sprinkler—the kind with a rotating arm that shoots water out in jets. When water flows out, the sprinkler spins in one direction due to Newton’s third law: for every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction. The water shooting out creates a force that pushes the sprinkler head the other way.

But what happens if you reverse the flow? Instead of spraying water, the sprinkler now sucks water in. Does it spin in the opposite direction? Does it spin at all? Does it simply sit still, perplexing everyone who looks at it?

For years, this "reverse sprinkler" problem has been a classic thought experiment in physics classrooms. Feynman himself, known for his brilliant intuition, reportedly wrestled with it. He built a device in his bathtub to test it, but even he found the results puzzling. The problem seemed simple, but the underlying fluid dynamics were surprisingly complex.

The Experiment That Finally Worked

The new research, led by a team at New York University, took a different approach. Instead of relying on theory alone, they built a precise, custom-designed sprinkler system submerged in a water tank. They used high-speed cameras and sensitive torque sensors to measure exactly what happened when water was sucked in.

“We wanted to settle this once and for all,” said one of the lead researchers. “The theoretical arguments were interesting, but they never quite matched up. We needed to see it for ourselves.”

What they found was a clear, unambiguous result. When water is sucked in, the reverse sprinkler does spin—but in the same direction as a normal sprinkler. That might sound counterintuitive, but the researchers observed it consistently. The spinning is also much weaker than the forward version, which explains why previous experiments were so hard to interpret.

Why It Took So Long

The reason the problem was so stubborn, the team explains, lies in the behavior of the water at the nozzle. When water sprays out, the force is strong and direct. When it’s sucked in, the water flows inward from all sides, creating a much more subtle effect. The team’s sensitive equipment was crucial for detecting the tiny, but real, torque.

“It’s a beautiful example of how careful measurement can resolve a long-standing puzzle,” the researcher added. “Sometimes, the simplest questions have the most surprising answers.”

The findings have been published in a peer-reviewed journal, and other physicists are already praising the work for its clarity and rigor. For Feynman fans, it’s a satisfying end to a decades-old mystery—one that proves even the greatest minds can be stumped by a humble sprinkler.
