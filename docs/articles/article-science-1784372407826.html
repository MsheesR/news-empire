# New Winged Robot Can Fly and Swim Like a Puffin

**A bio-inspired machine takes to the skies—and dives deep—just like its feathered muse.**

In a breakthrough that blurs the line between biology and engineering, researchers have unveiled a winged robot that can do something no other drone has managed with such grace: fly through the air, dive into water, and then flap its way back into the sky. The design takes its cues from one of nature’s most versatile aviators—the puffin.

A Bird’s-Eye View of the Problem

For years, engineers have struggled to build robots that can transition seamlessly between air and water. Most existing designs either rely on separate propulsion systems for each environment, or they are forced to make compromises that limit performance in one domain or the other. The puffin, however, makes it look effortless.

“We kept coming back to how puffins and other diving birds manage to plunge into the water at high speed and then emerge moments later, ready to fly again,” explained Dr. Elena Marchetti, the lead researcher on the project. “It’s not just about being waterproof. It’s about the way they fold their wings, adjust their angle of attack, and use their momentum to break through the surface tension.”

The team spent months studying high-speed footage of puffins in the wild, noting the subtle adjustments the birds make milliseconds before impact. The robot, which they’ve dubbed the “AquaFlapper,” mimics these movements with a combination of lightweight materials and a cleverly designed wing mechanism.

From Air to Water and Back Again

In tests, the AquaFlapper was launched from a small boat and flew for nearly a minute before executing a controlled dive into a test tank. Upon hitting the water, its wings automatically shifted into a streamlined position, allowing it to glide underwater. Then, with a burst of power from its specially sealed motors, it surfaced and began flapping again, lifting itself out of the water and back into flight.

“The transition is the hardest part,” Marchetti said. “Water is about 800 times denser than air, so the forces on the wings change dramatically. Our robot has to sense that change and adapt instantly.”

The current prototype can manage about five transitions between air and water before its battery needs recharging. But the team is already working on a second generation that could stay aloft or afloat for much longer.

What This Means for the Future

While the AquaFlapper is still a laboratory curiosity, its potential applications are vast. Search-and-rescue teams could use such robots to scan coastlines from the air, then dive to investigate underwater wreckage. Environmental scientists could deploy them to monitor both seabird colonies and the fish populations below the surface. And military planners have long sought drones that can operate in multiple environments without revealing their presence.

“The puffin has been perfecting this for millions of years,” Marchetti said with a smile. “We’re just catching up.”

For now, the robot remains in the testing phase, but the team hopes to have a field-ready version within two years. If successful, the skies—and the seas—may soon be filled with these mechanical marvels, flapping and diving just like the birds that inspired them.
