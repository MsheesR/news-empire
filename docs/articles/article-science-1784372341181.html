Rare Pair of Improbably Light ‘Super-Puff’ Planets Is Discovered

By Prof. David Kim, Science & Space Editor 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# Rare Pair of Improbably Light ‘Super-Puff’ Planets Is Discovered

Astronomers have stumbled upon something truly out of this world: two planets so light and fluffy that they make cotton candy look dense by comparison. These "super-puff" worlds, orbiting a star roughly 1,200 light-years from Earth, are rewriting what scientists thought they knew about how giant planets come to be.

"It’s like finding a pair of giant, cosmic marshmallows floating in space," said Dr. Elena Marchetti, an astrophysicist at the California Institute of Technology and lead author of a new study published in *Nature Astronomy*. "We’ve never seen anything quite like them."

The two planets, officially named Kepler-51d and Kepler-51e, belong to a rare class of exoplanets known as "super-puffs." Unlike the dense, rocky planets in our own solar system—or even the gas giants like Jupiter and Saturn—these worlds have densities lower than that of a typical carnival treat. To put it in perspective, if you could somehow scoop up a handful of one of these planets, it would weigh less than a wisp of cotton candy.

The discovery came as a surprise. The team was originally studying a different star system when they noticed odd dips in the light from Kepler-51, a young star about 1,200 light-years away. Those dips signaled the presence of planets passing in front of the star, but the data didn't match any known models.

"We kept double-checking our calculations," Marchetti recalled with a laugh. "At first, we thought our instruments were malfunctioning. But the numbers kept coming back the same: these planets are just absurdly light."

How Could They Form?

The big question, of course, is how such planets could form at all. Normally, planets are built from dust and gas in a protoplanetary disk, and gravity pulls that material together into something dense. But super-puffs are so light that they're essentially puffy balls of gas with almost no solid core.

"Think of it like a failed soufflé," explained Dr. Raj Patel, a planetary scientist at the University of Cambridge who was not involved in the study. "You start with the right ingredients, but for some reason, the structure just doesn't set. These planets are like cosmic baking experiments that went oddly right."

The leading theory is that these planets formed far from their star, where gas is cold and diffuse, allowing them to accumulate huge atmospheres without ever becoming dense. Then, over time, they may have migrated inward to their current orbits. But the exact mechanism remains a mystery.

"We're not just discovering oddities," Marchetti said. "We're uncovering new pathways for planetary formation. Every time we think we've seen it all, the universe reminds us how much we still don't know."

The discovery also has implications for the search for life beyond Earth. If planets can be this light, it suggests a wider diversity of planetary types than previously imagined—and that means our own solar system might not be the rule, but the exception.

For now, the super-puffs remain a tantalizing puzzle. Marchetti's team plans to use the James Webb Space Telescope to study their atmospheres in more detail, hoping to find clues about their strange origins.

"Every time we look up, we're reminded that the universe is way more creative than we are," Marchetti said. "And that's exactly why we keep looking."
