E.U. Proposes Changes to Emissions Trading System

By Prof. David Kim, Science & Space Editor 📅 2026-07-18 ⏱️ 3 min read

# E.U. Proposes Changes to Emissions Trading System

**Brussels —** The European Union is finding that the road to carbon neutrality is getting steeper, and the scenery isn't as popular as it once was. Facing mounting political pressure and what officials now acknowledge is a "green backlash," the European Commission has unveiled a series of proposed changes to its flagship Emissions Trading System (ETS), aiming to recalibrate its climate ambitions without alienating citizens and industries.

The Easy Cuts Are Gone

For years, the ETS worked quietly in the background. Power plants and heavy industries bought and sold permits to emit carbon dioxide, and the overall cap on pollution gradually tightened. It was a relatively painless way to show climate progress. But those days, officials admit, are over.

"The low-hanging fruit has been picked," one senior E.U. climate negotiator told me on condition of anonymity. "Now, every ton of CO2 we want to save requires a bigger overhaul — of factories, of heating systems, of entire supply chains. And people are feeling that pinch in their wallets."

The "pinch" is real. Carbon prices have soared, pushing up energy bills for households and production costs for manufacturers. In parts of Eastern Europe, where coal still dominates, the transition is seen less as an opportunity and more as an existential threat to livelihoods.

A Shift in Tone

The proposed changes reflect a subtle but significant shift in tone from Brussels. Instead of purely focusing on faster cuts, the Commission is now talking about "flexibility" and "social fairness." Key elements include a slower phase-out of free pollution permits for certain energy-intensive industries like steel and cement, and a new mechanism to cushion the financial blow for lower-income households.

"We cannot afford to lose the public's support," said a spokesperson for the European Commission during a briefing. "The green transition must be just, or it simply won't happen."

Industry Reacts with Caution

Initial reactions from industry groups have been cautiously optimistic. The steel sector, which has lobbied hard for continued free allowances, welcomed the "pragmatic approach." Environmental groups, however, were quick to condemn the move as a backslide.

"The E.U. is blinking," said a climate policy analyst with a major environmental NGO. "They are watering down the very tool that made the ETS a global model. This is a gift to polluters, not a path to net-zero."

What Comes Next

The proposed changes now head to the European Parliament and member states for negotiation — a process that can drag on for months. In the meantime, the political landscape is shifting. Far-right parties that campaigned on scrapping climate policies altogether gained ground in recent elections. Meanwhile, farmers have been blocking highways in protest of environmental regulations.

The E.U. is walking a tightrope. It still aims to be the world's first climate-neutral continent by 2050. But as one Brussels insider put it, "The destination hasn't changed. The question is whether the passengers are willing to stay on the bus."
